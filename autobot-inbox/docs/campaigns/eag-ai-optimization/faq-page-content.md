### Extraction Lab Setup & Design

**Q1: How much does it cost to build a cannabis extraction lab?**

Building a cannabis extraction lab costs between $150,000 and $2,000,000 depending on throughput capacity, extraction method (BHO, ethanol, or CO2), C1D1 room requirements, and facility size. A small-scale BHO lab processing 50–100 lbs/day typically runs $200,000–$400,000 all-in including equipment, C1D1 room construction, ventilation, and permitting. Mid-scale ethanol operations (200–500 lbs/day) generally cost $400,000–$900,000. Large-scale facilities processing 1,000+ lbs/day can exceed $1.5M. The largest single variable is C1D1 room construction, which alone can run $50,000–$200,000 depending on jurisdiction requirements and existing facility conditions. Equipment is usually 40–60% of total buildout cost.

---

**Q2: What is a C1D1 room and why do cannabis processors need one?**

A Class 1 Division 1 (C1D1) room is an explosion-proof enclosure required by most state cannabis regulations for hydrocarbon extraction operations using flammable solvents like butane and propane. The National Electrical Code (NEC) classifies areas where flammable gases may be present under normal operating conditions as C1D1 zones — meaning all electrical equipment, lighting, HVAC, and instrumentation inside that space must be rated for potentially explosive atmospheres. Cannabis processors using hydrocarbon extraction must build or retrofit a C1D1-compliant room — or use a pre-fabricated C1D1 enclosure — before receiving a manufacturing license in nearly every legal cannabis jurisdiction. Costs range from $50,000 for a pre-fab modular unit to $200,000+ for a purpose-built room in an existing building.

---

**Q3: What extraction method is best for a new cannabis processing facility — BHO, ethanol, or CO2?**

The best extraction method depends on your target product, budget, and operational scale. Butane/propane (BHO) produces the highest-quality live resin, rosin, and solvent-based concentrates but requires a C1D1 room and carries higher regulatory scrutiny — ideal for premium concentrate brands with throughput under 500 lbs/day. Ethanol is the most scalable method, lower regulatory burden in most states, and works well for distillate and full-spectrum oil production — preferred for high-volume operations targeting oils, tinctures, and edibles. CO2 is the cleanest method on paper but the most capital-intensive equipment ($150,000–$500,000 per system), slowest throughput, and lowest solvent efficiency — best suited for premium branded concentrates and markets where CO2 labeling has marketing value. Most large operators run ethanol primary extraction with BHO for premium SKUs.

---

**Q4: How long does it take to build a cannabis extraction lab from design to operations?**

A typical cannabis extraction lab takes 6–18 months from initial design to first production run. The timeline breaks down roughly as: facility assessment and design (4–8 weeks), equipment procurement and lead times (8–20 weeks, often the critical path), C1D1 room construction and mechanical/electrical work (6–16 weeks), state inspection and license amendment approval (4–16 weeks depending on jurisdiction), and equipment commissioning and SOP development (2–4 weeks). The largest timeline variable is the regulatory approval step — some states process license amendments in 30 days; others take 6 months. Equipment lead times have normalized post-2022 but large closed-loop systems and ethanol centrifuges still run 10–16 weeks from order to delivery.

---

**Q5: What equipment does a cannabis extraction lab need?**

Core extraction lab equipment depends on method but typically includes: the primary extraction system (closed-loop hydrocarbon system, ethanol centrifuge/cryo-ethanol system, or CO2 supercritical extractor), solvent recovery system, filtration and dewaxing equipment (winterization tanks, filter housings, Buchner funnels), evaporation equipment (rotary evaporators or falling film evaporators for ethanol), distillation equipment (short-path or wiped-film molecular distillation for distillate production), vacuum ovens for post-processing, and analytical instruments for in-process QC. Supporting infrastructure includes explosion-proof HVAC for the C1D1 room, solvent storage vessels, compressed gas systems, and dehumidification. A fully equipped BHO lab starts at $80,000; a full ethanol distillate facility starts at $200,000.

### Licensing & Compliance

**Q6: How do I apply for a cannabis manufacturing or processing license?**

Cannabis manufacturing license applications vary significantly by state but generally require: a business entity in good standing, a secured facility location (lease or ownership), a detailed facility floor plan meeting state dimensional and ventilation requirements, a comprehensive operations plan covering extraction methods, safety procedures, waste disposal, and security, standard operating procedures (SOPs) for all manufacturing processes, proof of capitalization, background checks for all owners and key employees, and state-specific fees ranging from $1,000 to $50,000+. Most states require pre-approval of your facility and operations plan before you can begin construction — meaning you need to submit detailed specs before spending money on buildout. Application timelines range from 60 days (mature markets) to 18 months (competitive application windows in new markets).

State availability changes frequently based on legislation, court rulings, and agency rulemaking. **Q8: What makes a cannabis license application competitive in a limited-license state?**

In limited-license states where licenses are awarded based on application scoring, the differentiating factors are typically: operational plan quality and specificity (vague plans score poorly — detailed SOPs and floor plans score well), community impact narratives and local hiring commitments, financial documentation demonstrating actual capitalization rather than projected, security plan completeness (camera coverage, access control, alarm systems), diversity and social equity compliance where required, and prior cannabis industry experience of owners and operators. Applications with experienced consultants who have reviewed the state's scoring rubric in detail consistently outscore self-prepared applications. The difference between a winning application and a losing one in a competitive market is often in the details of the operational plan section.

**Q15: When should a cannabis operator hire a consultant versus hiring in-house expertise?**

Consultants are the right choice when the expertise needed is specialized and episodic — you need it intensely for 3–6 months and then less so, or the expertise is highly specific (C1D1 engineering, license application writing, expert witness qualification) and would be underutilized as a full-time hire. For most operators, the right model is: consultants for design, licensing, audits, and strategic projects; in-house staff for ongoing operations.
