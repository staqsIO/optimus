## H2: C1D1 Room Requirements and Design

The C1D1 room is the most technically complex and most commonly misdesigned element of a hydrocarbon extraction facility. Getting it wrong delays your license, fails state inspection, or creates liability.

**What the NEC requires:** The National Electrical Code Article 501 governs Class 1 Division 1 electrical installations. All electrical equipment, luminaires, motors, switches, and instrumentation within the classified zone must be listed for Class 1 Division 1 use. This includes explosion-proof lighting, EX-rated outlets, intrinsically safe instrumentation, and HVAC systems designed to prevent solvent accumulation.

**What states add to NEC:** State cannabis regulations typically layer additional requirements on top of NEC minimums, including minimum air changes per hour (typically 10–15 ACH for continuous ventilation), emergency shut-off requirements tied to gas detection, maximum solvent storage limits within the processing area, secondary containment for solvent vessels, and inspector-specific documentation requirements that vary by jurisdiction.

**Common design mistakes:**
- Using general commercial HVAC rather than explosion-rated ventilation systems
- Installing non-EX-rated electrical panels or breakers inside or adjacent to the classified zone
- Under-sizing ventilation for the room volume and solvent volumes used
- Failing to classify adjacent spaces (corridors, storage areas) that may have solvent vapor exposure
- Selecting pre-fab C1D1 enclosures that are undersized for the extraction equipment specified

**Pre-fab vs. built-in-place:** Pre-fabricated modular C1D1 enclosures (from vendors like Extraction Tek, Iron Fist Fabrications, and others) cost $50,000–$120,000 and offer faster deployment — typically 8–12 weeks from order to installation. Built-in-place rooms in existing industrial buildings cost $80,000–$200,000 but can be designed to exact dimensions and offer more flexibility for large equipment. EAG evaluates both options for each client based on timeline, budget, and facility constraints.

## H2: Extraction Method Selection: BHO, Ethanol, and CO2

**Butane/Propane (BHO)** produces the highest-quality live resin, live rosin, and solvent-based concentrates in the premium market. BHO requires a C1D1 room, has the strictest state regulatory requirements, and operates at smaller throughput per system (most closed-loop systems process 1–10 lbs per run). BHO is the right choice for premium concentrate brands with throughput under 200–300 lbs/day raw material who prioritize product quality and retail price point over operational simplicity.

**Ethanol** is the most scalable extraction method for high-volume operations. Cryo-ethanol systems can process 200–1,000 lbs/hour with proper equipment. Ethanol is classified as a Class 1 Division 2 (C1D2) hazard in most states — a lower regulatory burden than C1D1, though still requiring appropriate ventilation and electrical classification. Ethanol is the preferred method for distillate production, full-spectrum oils, tinctures, and edible ingredients. Recovery rates and solvent costs make ethanol economics highly favorable at scale.

**CO2** (supercritical fluid extraction) is the most capital-intensive primary extraction method, with systems ranging from $150,000 to $500,000 per extractor. CO2 offers clean labeling and no solvent residue concerns but operates at 10–50 lbs/hour throughput — significantly slower than comparable ethanol systems. CO2 is best suited for premium oil brands where CO2 labeling has marketing value and throughput demands are moderate.

Most large multi-state operators run ethanol as the primary extraction method for volume products with BHO for premium concentrate lines. EAG recommends extraction method based on your product roadmap, not on equipment vendor relationships.

## H2: Common Mistakes in Extraction Lab Buildouts

EAG has reviewed dozens of extraction facilities and audited the results of designs done without experienced consulting input. The most expensive recurring mistakes:

**Undersizing for actual throughput:** Equipment is specced for current volume, not 12-month volume. Clients routinely find themselves at capacity within 6 months and unable to expand without significant capital reinvestment. EAG designs for planned throughput + 30% headroom.

**Contractor selection errors:** General contractors without cannabis extraction experience routinely underbid C1D1 room construction and fail to coordinate with state inspectors on documentation requirements. This produces failed inspections and costly rework. EAG maintains relationships with contractors experienced in cannabis manufacturing buildouts and can recommend vetted contractors in most major cannabis markets.

**Regulatory timeline underestimation:** Clients frequently plan their business timeline around construction completion, not regulatory approval. State license amendment processing times of 3–6 months in some jurisdictions mean that operations scheduled to begin in Month 6 often begin in Month 12. EAG builds regulatory timeline into project planning from day one.

**Equipment lead time failure:** High-demand extraction equipment — particularly ethanol centrifuges, falling film evaporators, and large CO2 systems — runs 10–16 weeks from order to delivery. Clients who wait to order equipment until facility construction is underway frequently sit in a completed facility waiting on equipment for 3–4 months. Equipment must be ordered at the start of the design phase, not at the end of construction.
