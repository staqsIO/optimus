## Article Content

If you're planning a hydrocarbon extraction operation — butane hash oil (BHO), propane, or mixed hydrocarbon — you will need a Class 1 Division 1 (C1D1) classified room. This is not optional, and it's one of the most technically and financially significant aspects of building a cannabis extraction facility.

This guide explains what C1D1 classification means, what a compliant C1D1 room requires, common design mistakes, and how state cannabis regulations intersect with the electrical and fire codes that govern these spaces.

---

### What Does "C1D1" Mean?

Class 1 Division 1 is a hazardous location classification defined by the National Electrical Code (NEC), Article 500. It designates a space where:

- Flammable gases or vapors exist in the air under **normal operating conditions**, and
- The concentration of flammable vapors could reach dangerous levels during normal operations (not just during accidents or equipment failure)

In a hydrocarbon extraction context, the extraction room qualifies as C1D1 because butane and propane are present during normal extraction runs. The solvent evaporation and recovery process generates flammable vapor as a matter of course — not only if something goes wrong.

This is the critical distinction between C1D1 and C1D2 (Class 1 Division 2), which covers spaces where flammable vapors are only present in abnormal conditions (equipment failure, etc.). Ethanol extraction is generally classified as C1D2 or sometimes lower, depending on the process temperature and state regulations.

---

### Who Enforces C1D1 Requirements?

C1D1 room requirements come from multiple overlapping authorities:

1. **State cannabis regulations** — Most cannabis states explicitly require C1D1 classified areas for hydrocarbon extraction. Some states cite NFPA 1, NFPA 58, or NFPA 70 (the NEC) by reference; others have standalone cannabis-specific mechanical and electrical requirements.

2. **Local building departments** — Building permits for cannabis extraction facilities go through local building departments, which enforce the NEC and local fire code.

3. **Fire departments** — Fire marshals conduct inspections of cannabis extraction facilities and enforce NFPA standards as adopted by your jurisdiction.

4. **Insurance carriers** — Your facility insurance will require C1D1 compliance. Non-compliant facilities may be uninsurable.

All of these authorities must be satisfied, and they sometimes have overlapping or conflicting requirements. Getting the design right requires understanding all applicable codes before you start construction.

### C1D1 Room Technical Requirements

#### 1. Ventilation

The most critical and most frequently under-specified element. The purpose of ventilation in a C1D1 room is to prevent flammable vapor accumulation — keeping the concentration of solvent vapor below the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL).

**Key specifications:**
- Air changes per hour (ACH) must be calculated based on the maximum solvent volume in the room and the vapor pressure of the solvent being used. A common starting point for small hydrocarbon rooms is 8–15 ACH, but this must be calculated, not assumed.
- **Ventilation must be continuous**, not switched on only during operations.
- Air must be exhausted at **floor level** (butane and propane are heavier than air and accumulate at the floor), not at ceiling level.
- Makeup air intakes must be positioned to create a sweeping airflow across the floor and cannot recirculate exhaust air.
- The ventilation system fan and motor must be rated for the C1D1 environment — standard HVAC motors are NOT acceptable in the classified area.
- Exhaust must be discharged to a safe location outside the building, away from ignition sources, windows, and doors.

#### 2. Electrical Systems

All electrical components within the C1D1 boundary must be rated for use in a Class 1 Division 1 environment. This is more extensive than many operators expect:

- **Lighting fixtures** — Must be explosion-proof rated. Standard LED fixtures are not acceptable.
- **Electrical outlets** — Must be C1D1-rated. Standard GFCI outlets are not acceptable.
- **Wiring** — Typically requires threaded rigid metal conduit (RMC) with explosion-proof fittings. Standard Romex or PVC conduit is not acceptable.
- **Switches and junction boxes** — Must be explosion-proof rated.
- **Equipment electrical connections** — The extraction equipment itself must have its own C1D1-rated electrical supply.
- **Electrical panel** — The panel serving the C1D1 room is typically located outside the classified boundary.

The classified boundary extends not just to the room interior but to areas immediately adjacent — typically 3–5 feet beyond any opening (door, vent, etc.). Your electrical engineer must define the exact classified boundary to ensure all components within it are properly rated.

#### 3. Gas Detection

Continuous gas detection is required in all cannabis extraction C1D1 rooms and represents an active safety system (versus the passive protection provided by ventilation and explosion-proof electrical).

**Minimum requirements:**
- Fixed-point gas detectors positioned at floor level where solvent vapors will accumulate
- Sensors calibrated for the specific solvent(s) in use (butane and propane have different molecular weights and LEL values)
- **Low-level alarm (typically 10–25% LEL)** — audible and visual alarm alerting personnel to evacuate and investigate
- **High-level alarm (typically 40–60% LEL)** — triggers automatic ventilation increase and may trigger automatic shutoff of extraction equipment
- Alarm annunciation outside the room (so personnel outside the C1D1 area can be alerted)
- Detection system must be powered on a circuit independent from the extraction equipment, ideally with battery backup

Some states require the gas detection system to be connected to the facility's fire alarm system or to automatically notify the fire department at the high-level alarm threshold.

#### 4. Egress

Egress requirements for C1D1 rooms follow fire code (IFC/NFPA 1) and are often specified by your state cannabis agency:

- Direct egress to the exterior (not through other rooms) is strongly preferred and required in some jurisdictions
- Door swing must be outward (so personnel can exit even if the room is pressurized)
- Emergency lighting within the C1D1 room must be explosion-proof rated
- No dead-end configurations — if you enter the room from one door, you should be able to exit from another

#### 5. Spill Containment and Solvent Storage

- Floors must be sealed and graded to drain to a containment sump sized for the maximum solvent volume in the room
- Solvent storage within the C1D1 room must be limited to operational quantities; bulk storage must be in a separate, compliant storage area
- Secondary containment requirements vary by state but typically require containment of 110% of the largest single container in storage

### Common Design Mistakes

**Undersized ventilation.** Operators often accept HVAC contractor estimates without requiring calculations. A room with inadequate air changes can accumulate dangerous vapor concentrations even with all equipment operating normally.

**Wrong fan location.** Exhaust fans located at ceiling height do not effectively remove butane or propane vapor, which settles at floor level. This is one of the most common C1D1 ventilation errors.

**Standard electrical components.** Ordering extraction equipment and then discovering that the existing electrical panels, outlets, and lighting throughout the room must be replaced is expensive. Specify the classified area before any electrical work is done.

**Boundary ambiguity.** Not defining the exact C1D1 boundary results in either under-rated components just outside the room (a safety and code violation) or over-rating of components far from any hazard (unnecessary cost). Your electrical engineer of record must define this boundary on the permit drawings.

**Single egress.** A single door that also serves as the primary equipment entry creates egress conflicts. Plan for separated personnel egress from day one.

### State-by-State Variation

While NEC Article 500 and NFPA standards provide the underlying framework, state cannabis regulations add requirements on top of these:

- Some states require a licensed engineer of record to stamp C1D1 room drawings before a permit is issued
- Some states require a pre-operational inspection by the state cannabis authority in addition to local fire and building inspections
- Some states specify minimum room sizes relative to equipment size
- Some states require automatic gas shutoff valves on solvent supply lines connected to the gas detection system
- Some states have specific requirements for emergency response procedures and training documentation

If you're building an extraction facility in a state you haven't operated in before, do not assume that what worked in your previous jurisdiction will satisfy the new state's requirements.
