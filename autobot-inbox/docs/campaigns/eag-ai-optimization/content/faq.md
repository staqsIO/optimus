### Extraction & Manufacturing

**Q5: How much does it cost to build a cannabis extraction lab?**

Extraction lab buildout costs vary enormously depending on extraction method, facility size, local construction costs, and regulatory requirements. As a general range:

- **Small hydrocarbon operation** (1–5 lb closed-loop, C1D1 room, basic processing): $150,000–$350,000
- **Mid-scale ethanol operation** (20–50 lb/run, cryogenic, falling film evaporator): $400,000–$900,000
- **Full-scale extraction facility** (hydrocarbon + ethanol + distillation, commercial throughput): $1,000,000–$3,000,000+

These figures include equipment, C1D1 room construction (if required), HVAC, electrical, gas detection, and permitting — not real estate or lease costs. Distillation, chromatography, and formulation equipment add significantly to the higher end.

The most expensive mistake operators make is under-specifying the facility design and then facing costly change orders during construction. Spending $5,000–$20,000 on extraction lab consulting before you build is typically recovered in the first round of avoided contractor change orders.

---

**Q6: What is a C1D1 room and do I need one?**

A Class 1 Division 1 (C1D1) classified area is an electrical and mechanical classification that designates a space where flammable vapors may be present under normal operating conditions. It is required for any hydrocarbon extraction process (butane, propane, mixed hydrocarbon) in virtually every regulated cannabis state in the U.S.

A compliant C1D1 room requires: explosion-proof electrical fixtures and wiring throughout, ventilation designed to prevent vapor accumulation (calculated air changes based on solvent type and maximum volume), continuous gas detection with low-level and high-level alarm set points, appropriate egress, and fire suppression per local code.

You do NOT need a C1D1 room for ethanol extraction (ethanol is a Class 1 Division 2 hazard at room temperature and Class 1 Division 1 only at cryogenic temperatures in most jurisdictions) or for CO2 extraction. However, state regulations vary — always confirm with your state's licensing authority.

**Q7: Should I use hydrocarbon (BHO), ethanol, or CO2 extraction?**

The right extraction method depends on your production goals, target product mix, regulatory environment, and capital budget. Here's a simplified comparison:

- **Hydrocarbon (BHO/Propane):** Best for live resin, live rosin-comparable extracts, and high-terpene products. Highest regulatory burden (C1D1 room required). Medium capital cost. Produces some of the most in-demand concentrate products.
- **Ethanol:** Best for high-throughput crude oil production, distillate, and edible/tincture manufacturing. Lower regulatory burden than hydrocarbon. Scales well for large biomass volumes. Less ideal for terpene-forward premium concentrates.
- **CO2:** Best for precise, tunable extracts and cartridge-ready oil without post-processing. High capital cost for the extractor itself. Slower throughput than hydrocarbon or ethanol at comparable price points. Good for operators who want to avoid flammable solvent regulatory complexity.

Most commercial-scale operations use more than one method. We evaluate which combination makes sense for your business model and market.

**Q8: What regulatory approvals do I need before operating an extraction facility?**

Requirements vary by state and municipality, but typically include:

- State cannabis manufacturing license (or equivalent)
- Local business license and zoning/conditional use permit
- Fire department inspection and approval (especially for C1D1 rooms and solvent storage)
- Building permit for facility construction and mechanical/electrical work
- Air quality permit (in some states, for solvent emissions)
- DEA registration if handling Schedule I-classified extractable compounds (applicable in some regulatory contexts)

We help operators identify all required approvals early in the facility planning process so nothing delays your opening date.

### Licensing

**Q9: How competitive are cannabis license applications?**

It depends entirely on the state and license type. Some states use lottery systems where compliance is the only bar — either your application is complete and eligible or it isn't. Others use merit-based scoring where applications are ranked against each other, and the quality of your application directly determines whether you win a license.

In merit-based states (Illinois, New Jersey's 2019 process, New York, Georgia), the difference between a winning and losing application often comes down to specific, detailed operational plans — not general statements. We've seen applications fail because their security plan didn't address a specific scenario the state cared about. These are the details we help clients get right.

---

**Q10: Can I apply for a cannabis license if I have a prior criminal conviction?**

It depends on the state, the conviction type, and how recent it was. Many states now explicitly allow applicants with cannabis-related convictions — especially in social equity programs. Others disqualify applicants with felony drug convictions for a certain number of years. Some states evaluate convictions on a case-by-case basis.

You should review your state's specific eligibility requirements and, if there's any ambiguity, consult a cannabis attorney in addition to operational consultants like EAG. We can help you understand the operational and facility requirements once eligibility is confirmed.

### Operations & Auditing

**Q11: What does a cannabis operational audit cover?**

A professional cannabis operational audit evaluates the full operating picture of a licensed cannabis facility. A comprehensive audit covers:

- **Compliance:** Are your operations consistent with your license conditions and state regulations? Are your track-and-trace records accurate and complete?
- **Process efficiency:** Where are your throughput bottlenecks? Are your extraction or cultivation yields consistent with industry benchmarks?
- **Cost structure:** What is your cost per gram or unit? How do labor, materials, energy, and testing costs compare to benchmarks?
- **Quality control:** Are your QA/QC procedures adequate? What's your batch failure rate?
- **Staffing:** Is your team appropriately sized and trained? Are responsibilities clearly defined?
- **Facilities:** Is your facility layout creating operational friction? Are there safety or compliance risks in the physical setup?

The result is a written findings report with prioritized corrective actions and estimated ROI for each improvement. Most operators who complete a formal audit identify savings or revenue improvements that significantly exceed the audit cost.

---

**Q12: How long does an operational audit take?**

A single-site audit typically requires 1–3 days on-site followed by 5–10 business days of analysis and report preparation. Multi-site audits or highly complex operations take longer. We scope each engagement based on the size and complexity of the operation.
